Greetings

Kumusta

English:

Hello

Chinese:

妳好

Hello

"Kumusta" is a common Filipino greeting that translates to "How are you?" It is derived from the Spanish phrase "¿Cómo está?" This greeting reflects the warm and hospitable nature of Filipino culture, where asking about someone's well-being is a way to show care and establish connection. It's customary to respond with "Mabuti" (Good) or "Ayos lang" (It's okay) to continue the friendly exchange.

This exchange highlights the warmth and hospitality that Filipinos are known for in their interactions with others.

Washroom

In the Philippines, "palikuran" (toilet) often refers to a variety of facilities, ranging from simple pit latrines in rural areas to modern restrooms in urban settings. Interestingly, many Filipino homes have a unique feature called "tabo," a water dipper traditionally used for cleaning after using the toilet, reflecting the country's emphasis on hygiene.

The Philippines has a rich tradition of education, with the "paaralan" (school) system incorporating both formal and informal learning. Notably, the country celebrates National Teachers' Day on October 5, honoring the vital role of educators in shaping the future of Filipino youth. Additionally, the "Buwan ng Wika" (Language Month) is celebrated every August to promote the use of Filipino language and culture in schools.

Home

The traditional "bahay" (house) in the Philippines often features unique architectural styles, such as the "Bahay Kubo," a nipa hut made of bamboo and palm leaves. This type of house is elevated to protect against flooding and allows for ventilation, making it ideal for the tropical climate. Additionally, many Filipino homes incorporate communal spaces to foster family and community bonding.

Landmarks

Intramuros

English:

Walled city

Chinese:

西班牙王城

intramuros

"Intramuros" is a historic walled city in Manila, Philippines. Its name means "within the walls" in Spanish, and it served as the center of Spanish colonial government from the 16th to the 19th centuries. Intramuros is known for its well-preserved architecture, including Fort Santiago and San Agustin Church, which showcase the country's rich history and cultural heritage. Today, it is a popular tourist destination, offering a glimpse into the Philippines' colonial past through its cobblestone streets, museums, and historical landmarks.

Liwasang Rizal

English:

Rizal park

Chinese:

黎剎公園

liwasang_rizal

"Liwasang Rizal," or Rizal Park, is a famous historical urban park located in Manila, Philippines. Named after the national hero, Dr. José Rizal, the park is a significant site that commemorates his life and contributions to the country's fight for independence. The park features a monument dedicated to Rizal, gardens, a lagoon, and various sculptures. It serves as a popular gathering place for locals and tourists alike, hosting events, celebrations, and cultural activities. Rizal Park symbolizes national pride and the importance of remembering the sacrifices made for freedom.

Mactan Shrine

English:

Liberty Shrine

Chinese:

馬克坦聖地

Three

The Mactan Shrine is a historical landmark located in Mactan Island, Cebu, Philippines. It commemorates the Battle of Mactan, where the local chieftain Lapu-Lapu and his warriors fought against the Spanish conquistador Ferdinand Magellan on April 27, 1521. The shrine features a statue of Lapu-Lapu, symbolizing Filipino bravery and resistance against colonization. The site includes a small museum that provides information about the battle and its significance in Philippine history, making it an important cultural and tourist destination that celebrates Filipino heritage and nationalism.

Folklores

Aswang Myth

English:

Shapeshifting monster

Chinese:

阿旺神話

aswang_myth

The Aswang is a popular figure in Philippine folklore, often depicted as a shape-shifting monster that preys on the living. This mythical creature is said to have various forms, including that of a vampire, witch, or ghoul, and is often associated with night-time terror. Aswang legends vary across different regions of the Philippines, with some portraying them as malevolent beings that steal babies or cause illness. The myth of the Aswang serves as a cautionary tale, reflecting cultural fears and societal norms, and is frequently featured in literature, film, and local festivals, continuing to capture the imagination of both Filipinos and international audiences.

Kanlaon

English:

Mount Kanlaon

Chinese:

坎拉翁火山

kanlaon

Kanlaon is an active volcano located on Negros Island in the Philippines. It is the highest peak in the Visayas region, standing at about 2,465 meters (8,747 feet) above sea level. Kanlaon is not only a natural wonder but also a significant cultural symbol. It is often associated with various local legends and is considered sacred by some indigenous communities. The volcano is part of the Kanlaon Natural Park, which is rich in biodiversity, featuring lush forests, unique wildlife, and stunning landscapes. It attracts hikers, nature enthusiasts, and researchers, making it a vital destination for eco-tourism in the Philippines.

Bakunawa

English:

Moon dragon

Chinese:

巴庫那瓦

Three

The Bakunawa is a legendary sea serpent in Philippine mythology, often depicted as a dragon-like creature that is believed to cause eclipses by swallowing the moon. According to folklore, the Bakunawa was once a beautiful being that transformed into a monstrous serpent due to its desire to consume the moon's beauty. This myth reflects cultural beliefs about celestial phenomena and has been passed down through generations. In some versions of the story, people would make loud noises, such as beating pots and pans, to scare the Bakunawa away and restore the moon. The tale of Bakunawa symbolizes the interplay between nature and mythology in Filipino culture.
